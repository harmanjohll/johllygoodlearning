# PART 1: SITUATIONAL WRITING (30 marks)

---

## What Is Situational Writing?

You are given a SITUATION and asked to write a functional piece of writing. You must:
1. Use the correct FORMAT for the text type
2. ADDRESS all the bullet points given in the task
3. Use the correct REGISTER (formal or informal tone)

**Key principle:** If you miss even ONE bullet point, you lose approximately 3–5 marks immediately.

---

## The Seven Text Types

### 1. FORMAL LETTER

**When it is used:** Writing to a company, organisation, school, or someone you do not know personally.

**Format:**

```
[Your address — top right]
[Date — below your address]

[Recipient's name/title and address — left side]

Dear Sir/Madam,  OR  Dear Mr/Mrs/Ms [Surname],

[Paragraph 1: State purpose of writing]
[Paragraph 2–3: Address the task bullet points]
[Final paragraph: Closing statement]

Yours faithfully,    ← use when you wrote "Dear Sir/Madam"
Yours sincerely,     ← use when you wrote "Dear Mr/Mrs [Name]"
[Full name]
```

**Register:** Formal — no contractions (don't, can't), no slang, polite and professional

**Sample opening lines:**
- "I am writing to express my concern about..."
- "I am writing with regard to..."
- "I am writing to enquire about..."
- "I am writing to complain about..."

---

### 2. INFORMAL LETTER OR EMAIL

**When it is used:** Writing to a friend, relative, or someone you know well.

**Format (Informal Letter):**
```
[Your address — top right]
[Date — below your address]

Dear [First name],

[Paragraph 1: Greeting and reason for writing]
[Paragraph 2–3: Address the task bullet points]
[Final paragraph: Closing remark]

Yours truly, / With love, / Best wishes,
[Your first name]
```

**Format (Informal Email):**
```
To: [email address]
From: [your email]
Subject: [short, clear subject line]

Dear [First name],

[Body paragraphs]

Best wishes,
[Your name]
```

**Register:** Friendly, conversational — contractions allowed, but still grammatically correct.

**Sample opening lines:**
- "How have you been? I hope you are keeping well."
- "It has been so long since we last spoke!"
- "I am writing to tell you about the most exciting thing that happened to me recently."

---

### 3. REPORT

**When it is used:** Reporting to a teacher, school principal, or organisation. Usually to describe an event, problem, or finding.

**Format:**
```
REPORT ON [TOPIC]
Prepared by: [Your name/role]
Date: [Date]

1. Introduction
[Why this report is written]

2. Findings / Observations
[Bullet points or numbered list of key information from the task]

3. Recommendations / Suggestions
[What should be done]

4. Conclusion
[Brief summary]

Submitted by: [Name]
```

**Register:** Formal, objective, impersonal ("It was observed that...", "It is recommended that...")

---

### 4. SPEECH

**When it is used:** Speaking to an audience — school assembly, debate, event opening.

**Format:**
```
Good morning/afternoon, [audience — e.g., teachers and fellow students],

[Paragraph 1: Greet audience and introduce yourself if needed]
[Paragraph 2–3: Main content — address the bullet points]
[Final paragraph: Call to action or closing statement]

Thank you.
```

**Register:** Formal but engaging — may use rhetorical questions, direct address ("You"), repetition for effect.

**Sample opening lines:**
- "Good morning, teachers and fellow students. Today, I would like to talk to you about..."
- "Have you ever wondered why...? This morning, I am here to explore this question with you."

---

### 5. EMAIL (FORMAL)

**When it is used:** Contacting a business, teacher, or organisation you do not know.

**Format:**
```
To: [email address]
From: [your email]
Subject: [clear subject]

Dear Sir/Madam / Dear Mr/Ms [Surname],

[Body — same as formal letter]

Yours faithfully / Yours sincerely,
[Full Name]
```

---

### 6. REVIEW

**When it is used:** Reviewing a book, film, event, or product.

**Format:**
```
REVIEW: [Title/Event Name]

Introduction: [What is being reviewed; brief background]
Summary: [Brief overview without spoilers]
Strengths: [What was good]
Weaknesses: [What could be improved — if asked]
Recommendation: [Would you recommend it? Why?]

Overall rating: [stars or a sentence]
```

**Register:** Semi-formal to informal. May use first person ("I felt...", "In my opinion...").

---

### 7. NOTICE / ANNOUNCEMENT

**When it is used:** Informing members of a group about an event or important information.

**Format:**
```
NOTICE

[TITLE OF NOTICE IN CAPITALS]

[Date]

[Body: who, what, when, where, why]

[Contact information if applicable]

[Name / Title of person who issued notice]
```

**Register:** Clear, concise, formal. No personal pronouns.

## Model Situational Writing: Formal Letter

**Task:** You are a student at Sunrise Primary School. You recently visited the new community library with your class. Write a formal letter to the library director, Mr Tan:
- Thanking him for hosting the visit
- Sharing two things you found most impressive
- Suggesting one improvement for future visits

---

**Model Answer:**

```
12 Sunrise Avenue
Singapore 520012
10 May 2026

Mr Tan Wei Liang
Director
Riverside Community Library
45 Riverside Walk
Singapore 238001

Dear Mr Tan,

I am writing on behalf of my class at Sunrise Primary School to express our 
sincere gratitude for hosting our visit to the Riverside Community Library on 
5 May 2026. It was an enriching and memorable experience for all of us.

We were particularly impressed by two aspects of the library. First, the 
Maker Space on the third floor was fascinating. The hands-on activities, 
especially the robotics station, sparked our curiosity and showed us that 
learning can be both fun and creative. Second, the dedicated Reading Garden 
on the rooftop provided a peaceful and calming environment that truly 
encouraged a love for reading.

However, we noticed that the guided tour was quite rushed, leaving us little 
time to explore each section thoroughly. We would like to suggest that future 
tours be extended to at least two hours to allow students more time to engage 
with the resources available.

Once again, thank you for your hospitality and for sharing your passion for 
literature with us. We hope to visit again in the near future.

Yours sincerely,
[Student's Full Name]
Class Monitor, P6 Integrity
Sunrise Primary School
```

**Annotations:**
- [Format correct]: address, date, salutation, sign-off all present
- [Bullet point 1 addressed]: "express our sincere gratitude" — thanking
- [Bullet point 2 addressed]: Two things — Maker Space and Reading Garden, both with specific detail
- [Bullet point 3 addressed]: Suggests extending the tour to two hours
- [Register]: Formal throughout — no contractions, polite phrases
- [Language]: Precise vocabulary ("enriching", "sparked our curiosity", "dedicated")

---

## Situational Writing Quick Checklist

Before submitting, check:
- [ ] Is the format correct (salutation, sign-off, heading)?
- [ ] Have I addressed ALL bullet points?
- [ ] Is the register appropriate (formal/informal)?
- [ ] Is my opening sentence purposeful (states why I am writing)?
- [ ] Is my closing sentence complete?
- [ ] No spelling or grammar errors?

# PART 2: CONTINUOUS WRITING (40 marks)

---

## Overview

You choose ONE from:
- 3 topic titles (write a story, narrative, or recount)
- 1 picture prompt (write a story inspired by the picture)

**Word count:** 150–300 words
**Advice:** Aim for 200–250 words. Quality matters more than length.

---

## Marks Breakdown

| Criteria | Marks | What Is Assessed |
|----------|-------|-----------------|
| Content | 20 | Ideas, relevance, development, vivid details, interest |
| Language | 15 | Vocabulary range, grammar accuracy, style, sentence variety |
| Organisation | 5 | Paragraphing, opening, ending, overall flow |
| **Total** | **40** | |

---

## Step 1: Choose Your Topic Wisely (3 minutes)

When you see the three topic options:
1. Choose the topic you can write MOST SPECIFICALLY about
2. Avoid vague topics unless you have a very clear story idea
3. Ask: Do I have a clear character, conflict, and resolution for this?

**If using the picture prompt:**
- Look at the picture for 1 minute
- What story does it suggest? Who is there? What just happened? What will happen next?
- You do not have to describe the picture literally — use it as inspiration

---

## Step 2: Plan Before You Write (5 minutes)

**Use this simple planning grid:**

| Element | Your Answer |
|---------|-------------|
| Who is my main character? | (Name, age, personality trait) |
| Where does the story take place? | (Specific setting: not just "a park" but "a deserted park at dusk") |
| What is the problem/conflict? | (Internal — character's fear/decision OR External — event/obstacle) |
| What is the turning point? | (The moment everything changes) |
| How does it resolve? | (How the problem is solved or what the character learns) |
| What is the message/feeling? | (What should the reader feel at the end?) |

---

## Step 3: The Story Arc

Every good PSLE story follows this structure:

```
1. OPENING         — Hook the reader; introduce character and setting (1 paragraph)
2. BUILD-UP        — Establish the situation; develop character/mood (1 paragraph)
3. CONFLICT/CLIMAX — The main problem; the most intense moment (2 paragraphs)
4. RESOLUTION      — How the problem is resolved (1 paragraph)
5. REFLECTION      — Character's thoughts/feelings after; lesson learned (1 paragraph)
```

**At 200-250 words, aim for approximately 5–6 paragraphs of 3–5 sentences each.**

---

## Step 4: Five Types of Engaging Openings

The opening is the first thing the examiner reads. A strong opening creates immediate interest.

### Opening Type 1: In Medias Res (Start in the Middle of Action)
Begin with the most dramatic moment. Pull the reader straight into the action.

Weak: "It was a sunny day and I went to the park with my family."
Strong: "The moment the rope snapped, I knew I was in serious trouble."

### Opening Type 2: Dialogue
Start with a character speaking. Creates immediacy and voice.

Weak: "My friend called me and told me about the problem."
Strong: "Quick! The river is rising!" my friend gasped, grabbing my arm and pulling me towards higher ground."

### Opening Type 3: Descriptive Setting
Immerse the reader in the atmosphere of the place.

Weak: "The forest was dark and scary."
Strong: "Twisted roots curled like gnarled fingers across the forest floor. The only sound was the rustling of unseen creatures in the undergrowth."

### Opening Type 4: Reflective Statement
Begin with a thought or lesson, then tell the story that led to it.

Weak: "I learned an important lesson one day."
Strong: "I used to think that apologising was a sign of weakness. I was wrong."

### Opening Type 5: Sensory Description
Use one or more senses to anchor the reader in the scene.

Weak: "I smelled something burning."
Strong: "The acrid smell of smoke hit me before I even opened the front door."

---

## Step 5: Showing vs Telling

This is the difference between average and excellent writing.

**Telling** says what happened. **Showing** makes the reader experience it.

| Telling (Weak) | Showing (Strong) |
|---------------|-----------------|
| She was very scared. | Her hands trembled and her mouth went dry. Each shadow seemed to shift and leer at her. |
| He was very happy. | A grin broke across his face and he let out a whoop of joy, pumping his fist in the air. |
| It was very hot. | The pavement shimmered in the heat. Sweat trickled down his back with every step. |
| She walked slowly. | She shuffled forward, shoulders hunched, dragging her feet as if they were made of lead. |
| He was nervous. | He cleared his throat three times. His palms left damp prints on the desk as he waited. |

**The key question to ask yourself:** Am I telling the reader how the character feels, or am I showing them evidence so they can FEEL it themselves?

---

## Step 6: Sentence Variety

Examiners reward students who vary sentence length and structure.

**Rule of thumb:** Mix short, punchy sentences with longer, descriptive ones.

**Example (poor):**
"I ran through the forest. There were many trees. It was getting dark. I was very scared. I could hear footsteps."

**Example (strong):**
"I ran. Branches whipped at my face and roots snatched at my feet, but I did not stop. Behind me — closer now — I could hear footsteps. Someone was following me."

**Sentence starters to vary your writing:**
- Start with an adverb: "Suddenly, the lights went out."
- Start with a verb+ing: "Gasping for breath, she reached the top of the hill."
- Start with a time phrase: "Before she could react, the glass slipped from her fingers."
- Start with a short exclamation: "It was too late."
- Use a rhetorical question: "Had she made the right choice?"

## Step 8: Descriptive Techniques (Figurative Language)

Using figurative language shows the examiner you have a strong command of English.

### Simile
Compare using "like" or "as":
- "Her voice was as cold as steel."
- "He moved like a shadow through the crowd."

### Metaphor
Direct comparison (say X IS Y):
- "The examination room was a battlefield."
- "Her laugh was music to his ears."

### Personification
Give human qualities to non-human things:
- "The wind whispered through the trees."
- "The old building groaned under the weight of years."

### Alliteration
Repeat initial consonant sounds for rhythm and effect:
- "The soft, silver moonlight spilled through the window."

### Rule of Three
Group three descriptive words or phrases for rhythm and impact:
- "She was exhausted, desperate, and completely alone."
- "He searched the room, checked under the bed, and rifled through every drawer."

## Step 9: Common Composition Topics and Vocabulary

### Theme: Friendship

**Key situations:** Misunderstanding with a friend, helping a friend in need, making a new friend, losing a friendship, reconciliation.

**Vocabulary:** loyal, trustworthy, reconcile, remorse, cherish, forgive, rift, misunderstanding, apologise, empathy, solidarity

**Model sentence:** "It was only when I saw the tears welling in her eyes that I understood the magnitude of my words and felt a wave of remorse wash over me."

---

### Theme: Courage / Overcoming Fear

**Key situations:** Standing up for someone, facing a fear, making a difficult choice, helping someone in danger.

**Vocabulary:** courage, determination, resolve, hesitation, apprehensive, trembling, gather strength, take a deep breath, overcome, victory, pride

**Model sentence:** "Every instinct told me to run, but I forced myself to stand my ground, my fists clenched at my sides."

---

### Theme: Environment / Nature

**Key situations:** Experiencing a storm, getting lost in nature, saving an animal, a flood, discovering a beautiful natural place.

**Vocabulary:** canopy, undergrowth, cascading, torrential, parched, verdant, serene, lush, gnarled, desolate

**Model sentence:** "The storm descended without warning — one moment the sky was a brilliant blue, the next, bruised clouds rolled in and the wind rose to a howl."

---

### Theme: Family

**Key situations:** An argument with a parent, a grandparent's lesson, caring for a sibling, a family celebration, helping at home.

**Vocabulary:** cherish, sacrifice, gratitude, resent, appreciate, reconcile, nurture, protective, devoted, understanding

**Model sentence:** "It was only when I saw the grey at my grandmother's temples and the tired lines around her eyes that I realised how much she had given, and how little I had given back."

---

### Theme: Unexpected Discovery / Adventure

**Key situations:** Finding something strange, getting lost, exploring an unfamiliar place, discovering a secret.

**Vocabulary:** stumble upon, peculiar, bizarre, uncanny, mystifying, exhilarating, trepidation, venture, curiosity, encounter

**Model sentence:** "There, half-buried beneath the roots of the old banyan tree, was a small metal box covered in rust."

## Model Composition (Annotated)

**Topic:** Write a story about a time when you had to make a difficult choice.

---

**Model:**

The moment I saw the wallet lying on the pavement, I stopped dead in my tracks. [In medias res opening — immediate tension]

It was a Tuesday afternoon, and the school corridor had long emptied. The worn leather wallet was thick with cash — I could see the edges of several notes poking out. I glanced around; there was no one in sight. A familiar voice in my head whispered that this was my chance. For weeks, I had been saving up to buy the new football boots I needed for the school team trials. Here, perhaps, was the solution. [Build-up — character desire established, moral dilemma introduced]

I reached down and picked up the wallet. Inside, along with the money, was an identity card — and a photograph. It was an elderly man, his arm around a young girl who wore the same school uniform as I did. Something lurched in my chest. This money mattered to someone. Perhaps it was all this person had. My fingers curled tightly around the wallet, then slowly uncurled. [Conflict — SHOWING the character's inner struggle through physical action]

With a long, shuddering breath, I turned and walked towards the school office. The teacher on duty looked up as I placed the wallet on the counter. "I found this outside," I said quietly. [Climax/Resolution — action taken]

Walking home that evening, my pockets were no heavier than before, and I still did not have my football boots. But I held my head a little higher, and somehow, that felt worth more than anything money could buy. [Reflection — lesson learned, satisfying ending]

---

**Annotations:**
- 5 paragraphs, approximately 220 words
- Story arc: Opening (action) → Build-up (moral dilemma) → Conflict (internal struggle) → Resolution (decision) → Reflection (lesson)
- Showing vs Telling: "Something lurched in my chest" not "I felt sad"
- Varied sentences: Short punchy sentence "I stopped dead in my tracks" followed by longer sentences
- Strong ending: Links back to the choice made; leaves an emotional impression

## Composition Common Mistakes

| Mistake | How to Fix |
|---------|-----------|
| Story is too rushed (no development) | Plan before you write; give each part of the arc enough space |
| Story is too predictable | Add an unexpected turn; make the conflict personal and specific |
| Using the same word repeatedly | Use the vocabulary uplift table; vary word choices |
| No paragraphs | Indent each new paragraph; change paragraph for each new part of arc |
| Story goes off topic | Refer back to the title at least once in the story |
| Weak ending ("And we all lived happily ever after") | End with a reflection, a specific detail, or a meaningful feeling |
| Too many characters | Focus on 1–2 characters only |
| No sensory detail | Add at least one visual, sound, and feeling description |
| All sentences the same length | Mix short and long sentences deliberately |
| Stopped at 150 words | Aim for 200–250; expand with description and dialogue |

## Marking Rubric Summary (For Harman to Use)

### Content (20 marks)

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 17–20 | Engaging, well-developed, vivid details, relevant throughout, original ideas |
| 13–16 | Generally relevant, some development, interesting moments but uneven |
| 9–12 | Adequate ideas, limited development, some irrelevant parts |
| 5–8 | Thin ideas, mostly telling not showing, largely undeveloped |
| 1–4 | Very little relevant content, minimal attempt |

### Language (15 marks)

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 13–15 | Wide range of vocabulary and structures, varied sentences, very few errors |
| 10–12 | Good range, some variety, occasional errors that do not impede meaning |
| 7–9 | Limited range, mostly simple structures, errors present |
| 4–6 | Very limited vocabulary, frequent errors that sometimes impede meaning |
| 1–3 | Severely limited, pervasive errors, meaning often unclear |

### Organisation (5 marks)

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 5 | Clear paragraphing, excellent opening and ending, ideas flow logically |
| 4 | Mostly clear paragraphing, good opening or ending |
| 3 | Some paragraphing, opening or ending adequate |
| 1–2 | Poor or no paragraphing, no clear structure |
