## THE RACE FRAMEWORK — Use for Every Open-Ended Answer

RACE is the single most important strategy for open-ended comprehension questions. Use it for every answer worth 2 or more marks.

| Letter | Meaning | What to Write |
|--------|---------|---------------|
| R | Restate | Rephrase the question as the start of your answer |
| A | Answer | Give the direct answer |
| C | Cite Evidence | Quote from the text (use quote marks) |
| E | Explain | Explain HOW the quote supports your answer |

### RACE Example

**Question (2 marks):** "How did Marcus feel when he heard the news? Support your answer with evidence from the text."

**Weak Answer (1 mark):**
"He felt sad."

**Strong RACE Answer (2 marks):**
"Marcus felt devastated when he heard the news. This is shown when the text states 'his knees buckled beneath him and his vision blurred with tears', suggesting that the news was so overwhelming that his body could not cope with the grief."

**Why the RACE answer gets full marks:**
- R: "Marcus felt devastated when he heard the news" (restates question)
- A: "devastated" (direct answer with a precise word)
- C: "his knees buckled beneath him and his vision blurred with tears" (exact quote)
- E: "suggesting that the news was so overwhelming that his body could not cope" (explains the quote)

## COMPONENT 1: VISUAL TEXT COMPREHENSION (5 marks, Booklet A, MCQ)

> **Format change in 2025:** Visual Text moved from Booklet B (10-mark open-ended) to Booklet A as **5 MCQ items × 1 mark**. Same skills, MCQ format. The strategies below now produce MCQ-elimination thinking rather than written answers.

### What Is Visual Text?
A non-prose text like a poster, infographic, advertisement, brochure, or webpage screenshot. The text uses a combination of images, headings, bullet points, and short paragraphs.

### What It Tests
- Can you find specific information from a visual layout? (literal MCQ)
- Can you make inferences from what you see? (inferential MCQ)
- Can you explain the effect of language choices? (language-use MCQ)
- Can you identify the overall purpose / audience? (purpose MCQ)

### Step-by-Step Strategy

**Step 1: Read everything on the visual (1–2 minutes)**
- Read ALL text: headings, subheadings, captions, small print, labels, slogans
- Note the purpose: Is it persuading? Informing? Warning? Announcing?
- Note the audience: Is it for children? Parents? The public?

**Step 2: Identify the structure**
- What sections are there?
- What information is in each section?
- What does the image/graphic contribute?

**Step 3: Read each MCQ stem and underline key words**
- "According to the text" → literal: find the matching word/phrase
- "Why does the writer..." → language-use: think about effect on reader
- "What can you infer..." → inferential: think about what is implied, not stated
- "What is the main purpose..." → purpose: persuade / inform / warn / instruct

**Step 4: MCQ elimination**
- Cross out clearly wrong options.
- Of the remaining two, pick the one most specifically supported by the visual.
- Beware "almost right" distractors that change ONE detail (e.g., "two activities" when the visual lists three).

### Question Types

**Type 1: Literal — Find the Information**
- Stem: "According to the poster, the carnival is held in which two locations?"
- Strategy: Find the exact section; compare options against the text word-for-word; eliminate options that change a number, name, or date.

**Type 2: Inferential — What Does This Suggest?**
- Stem: "What can you infer about the target audience of this brochure?"
- Strategy: Look at language level, images, content, and tone — who would this appeal to?

**Type 3: Language Use — Why This Word/Phrase?**
- Stem: "The advertisement uses the phrase 'Don't miss out!' What effect does this have on the reader?"
- Strategy: Think about the feeling or reaction the writer WANTS to create (urgency, reassurance, excitement, fear).

**Type 4: Purpose — Why Was This Made?**
- Stem: "What is the main purpose of this infographic?"
- Strategy: Look at the overall message — persuade, inform, warn, instruct.

### Visual Text Common Pitfalls

| Pitfall | How to Avoid |
|---------|-------------|
| Only reading the main heading | Read ALL text including small print and captions |
| Falling for "almost right" distractors | Compare each option word-for-word against the visual |
| Confusing audience with purpose | Audience = who reads it; Purpose = what it tries to do |
| Picking the option that "sounds best" | Pick the option most specifically supported by visible evidence |

## COMPONENT 2: COMPREHENSION CLOZE (15 marks)

### What Is Comprehension Cloze?
A passage with 15 blanks. You write ONE word in each blank. 1 mark per blank.

### The Four Types of Clues

**Type 1: Grammar Clue**
The grammar structure tells you what part of speech is needed.
- "She _______ very quickly." → verb needed → ran / moved / walked
- "The _______ dog barked all night." → adjective needed → hungry / small / black

**Type 2: Context Clue**
The surrounding sentences tell you the meaning needed.
- "The weather was bitterly cold. The children _______ their coats tightly." → pulled / clutched / wrapped

**Type 3: Collocation Clue**
Certain words always go together.
- "make a _______ " → decision / speech / mistake (not "make a do")
- "burst into _______ " → tears / laughter / flames

**Type 4: Word Form Clue**
The word before/after tells you the correct form.
- "She was _______ at the magic trick." (was + adjective) → amazed / astonished
- "His _______ was remarkable." (his + noun) → performance / achievement

### Step-by-Step Strategy

**Step 1: Read the whole passage without filling in anything (2 minutes)**
Get the general meaning. What is this about?

**Step 2: Fill in what you are confident about first**
Skip difficult blanks. Get the easy ones first.

**Step 3: Return to difficult blanks**
- Read the sentence before AND after the blank
- What part of speech is needed?
- What meaning fits the context?
- Does the word "sound right" with the words around it?

**Step 4: Check each blank**
- Does the grammar work? (subject-verb agreement, tense, article)
- Does it make sense in context?
- Is it ONE word only?

### Important Rules

1. Write only ONE word per blank
2. The word must fit grammatically AND meaningfully
3. If the blank needs an adjective, a noun will not be accepted
4. Watch for tense consistency — if the passage is past tense, use past tense

### Common Cloze Traps

| Trap | Example | Solution |
|------|---------|---------|
| Two-word answer | Writing "very cold" in one blank | Only one word allowed |
| Wrong tense | Writing "runs" in a past-tense passage | Match the passage tense |
| Grammatically wrong | Writing an adjective where a verb is needed | Check part of speech |
| Misspelling a correct word | Writing "beleive" instead of "believe" | Half the cloze marks come from accuracy |

## COMPONENT 3: EDITING FOR SPELLING AND GRAMMAR (10 marks)

### Format
- A passage of about 20–25 lines
- 10 lines contain errors (indicative split: 5 spelling, 5 grammar — verify against SEAB PDF)
- Some lines have no error
- Task: Identify the wrong word and write the correct word

### Marks Awarded
1 mark for correctly identifying the wrong word AND writing the correct form.
If you identify the right line but write the wrong correction, you get 0 marks.

### Step-by-Step Strategy

**Step 1: Read the whole passage first**
Understand the meaning. This helps you spot words that "don't fit."

**Step 2: Go line by line**
Read each line carefully, watching for:
- Words that look or sound wrong
- Grammar that doesn't match (SVA, tense, article, pronoun)

**Step 3: For spelling errors — sound it out**
- Does the word look unusual?
- Is a silent letter missing? (knight, wrist, knife)
- Is a double letter missing? (tomorrow, necessary, immediately)
- Is it a commonly confused pair? (their/there/they're; your/you're; its/it's)

**Step 4: For grammar errors — check these six areas:**
1. Tense — is the verb form correct for the passage time?
2. Subject-verb agreement — does the verb match the subject?
3. Article — is a/an/the correct?
4. Pronoun — is the right pronoun used?
5. Adjective/adverb — is the right form used?
6. Preposition — is the correct preposition used?

### Common Spelling Errors at PSLE Level

| Misspelled | Correct | Memory Tip |
|-----------|---------|------------|
| recieve | receive | "I before E except after C" |
| beleive | believe | "lie" is inside believe |
| seperately | separately | "par" not "per" in the middle |
| occured | occurred | double r: oc-CUR-red |
| embarass | embarrass | double r AND double s |
| neccessary | necessary | 1 c, 2 s |
| tommorrow | tomorrow | 1 m, 2 r's: to-MOR-row |
| definately | definitely | def-IN-ite-ly |
| restaraunt | restaurant | restaurant has "aura" inside |
| dissapear | disappear | 1 s, 2 p's: dis-AP-pear |
| accomodate | accommodate | double c AND double m |
| begining | beginning | double n: be-GIN-ning |
| existance | existence | -ence not -ance |
| independant | independent | -ent not -ant |
| lisence | licence (noun) / license (verb) | -ce for noun in British English |

### Common Grammar Errors in Editing

| Error Type | Example Wrong | Example Correct |
|------------|--------------|-----------------|
| SVA | The birds was singing. | The birds were singing. |
| Tense | Yesterday, he runs home. | Yesterday, he ran home. |
| Article | She is a honest person. | She is an honest person. |
| Pronoun | Me and Jack played. | Jack and I played. |
| Double comparative | more taller | taller |
| -ed/-ing adjective | I am boring. | I am bored. |
| Apostrophe | its raining | it's raining |
| Irregular plural | The childs ran. | The children ran. |

### Editing Pitfalls to Avoid

| Pitfall | How to Avoid |
|---------|-------------|
| Changing a correct word | Only change a word if you are sure it is wrong |
| Changing the meaning | The correction must keep the sentence's original meaning |
| Writing two corrections | Only change ONE word per line |
| Misspelling your correction | Double-check your spelling before writing it |

## COMPONENT 4: COMPREHENSION OPEN-ENDED (20 marks)

> **Format change in 2025:** Booklet B has **ONE** comprehension passage (not two). The passage is **either narrative or non-narrative**; the genre rotates between years. ~10 questions, 20 marks total. The full taxonomy of question types below applies regardless of genre.

### Passage Type — Narrative
A story or creative non-fiction passage. Tests your ability to understand character, plot, emotion, and language.

### Passage Type — Non-Narrative
An informational, explanatory, or argumentative passage. Tests your ability to understand facts, opinions, arguments, and how information is organised.

### Recognising which genre you have

| Cue | Narrative | Non-Narrative |
|---|---|---|
| First paragraph opens with… | A character, a scene, a feeling | A claim, a fact, a question |
| Tense | Mostly past simple | Mostly present simple |
| Dialogue | Common | Rare |
| Imagery and figurative language | Heavy | Limited |
| Structure | Story arc | Argument or information flow |
| Likely question slant | Character, emotion, language craft | Author's argument, text organisation, evaluative |

## Question Type Deep Dive

### Type 1: Literal Questions (1–2 marks)

**What they ask:** "According to the text...", "What did [character] do when...", "List two reasons..."

**Strategy:**
1. Find the relevant paragraph (scan for keywords from the question)
2. Locate the exact answer in the text
3. Write in your own words (don't just copy — unless quoting)
4. For 2 marks: give 2 distinct points

**Example:**
- Question: "What were two signs that the storm was approaching? (2 marks)"
- Wrong: "The sky and the birds" (too vague)
- Correct: "Two signs that the storm was approaching were the darkening of the sky and the sudden silence of the birds." (specific, complete)

---

### Type 2: Inferential Questions (2–3 marks)

**What they ask:** "Why do you think...", "What does this suggest about...", "How do you know that..."

**Key rule:** Your answer must be supported by evidence from the text. Never infer something you cannot link back to the passage.

**Strategy:**
1. Ask: What is the character feeling/thinking? Why is the event happening?
2. Find text evidence that supports your inference
3. Use RACE: State your inference + quote + explain

**Example:**
- Question: "How do you know that Priya was nervous about her performance? Support your answer with evidence. (2 marks)"
- Correct: "Priya was nervous about her performance. This is evident from the text's description that 'she clasped and unclasped her hands repeatedly', which suggests she was anxious and could not keep still as the time for her performance drew near."

---

### Type 3: Vocabulary-in-Context (1–2 marks)

**What they ask:** "What does the word '_____' mean as used in the passage?", "Find a word in paragraph 3 that means 'extremely tired'."

**Strategy:**
1. Read the WHOLE sentence containing the word — do not just look at the word in isolation
2. Read the sentence before and after it for additional context
3. Think: What meaning would make sense here?
4. Suggest a synonym that fits the context

**Example:**
- Question: "What does 'ravenous' mean as used in paragraph 2? (1 mark)"
- Passage context: "After the long hike, the children were ravenous and could not wait to sit down to eat."
- Correct: "Ravenous means extremely hungry." OR "Ravenous means famished." (1 mark)

> Tip: Never define vocabulary with the same root word. "Ravenous means to raven" would get 0 marks.

---

### Type 4: Language Use / Writer's Craft (2 marks)

**What they ask:** "What does the phrase '______' suggest about [character/situation]?", "Explain the effect of the writer's choice to describe [X] as [Y].", "Why does the writer use the word '______'?"

**Strategy (always two-part):**
1. Identify the TECHNIQUE (simile, metaphor, personification, alliteration, repetition, etc.)
2. Explain the EFFECT — what feeling/idea does it create for the reader?

**Formula:** "The writer uses [technique] to [effect/suggest something about character/situation]. This creates a sense of [emotion/atmosphere] in the reader."

**Example:**
- Question: "What is the effect of the phrase 'the wind howled like a wounded animal'? (2 marks)"
- Correct: "The writer uses a simile, comparing the wind's sound to that of a wounded animal, to suggest that the wind is both powerful and unsettling. This creates a sense of menace and danger, making the reader feel uneasy about what is about to happen."

**Common language techniques at PSLE level:**

| Technique | Definition | Example |
|-----------|-----------|---------|
| Simile | Comparison using like/as | as brave as a lion |
| Metaphor | Direct comparison (X is Y) | His voice was thunder. |
| Personification | Giving human qualities to non-human things | The sun smiled down. |
| Alliteration | Repetition of initial consonant sounds | softly, silently, smoothly |
| Onomatopoeia | Words that sound like what they describe | crash, sizzle, whisper |
| Hyperbole | Deliberate exaggeration | I've told you a million times. |
| Repetition | Repeating a word/phrase for emphasis | She waited, and waited, and waited. |
| Imagery | Language that appeals to the senses | the sharp smell of rain on dry earth |

---

### Type 5: Character / Emotions Questions (2 marks)

**What they ask:** "How did [character] feel about...?", "Describe [character]'s attitude towards..."

**Strategy:**
1. Identify the emotion with a PRECISE word (not just "happy", "sad", "scared")
2. Find evidence — what did the character do, say, or think that shows this?
3. Use RACE

**Emotion Vocabulary Bank (upgrade from weak to strong):**

| Weak | Strong Alternatives |
|------|-------------------|
| happy | elated, overjoyed, jubilant, content, relieved |
| sad | devastated, heartbroken, dejected, forlorn, crestfallen |
| scared | terrified, petrified, apprehensive, alarmed, anxious |
| angry | furious, livid, indignant, seething, exasperated |
| surprised | astonished, flabbergasted, bewildered, taken aback |
| confused | perplexed, baffled, puzzled, disoriented |
| proud | triumphant, gratified, elated, satisfied |
| tired | exhausted, drained, weary, haggard |

---

### Type 6: Author's Purpose / Main Idea (2 marks)

**What they ask:** "What is the main idea of this passage?", "Why did the writer include paragraph 4?", "What is the writer's purpose in this article?"

**Strategy:**
1. For main idea: summarise the overall message in one sentence
2. For writer's purpose: identify the effect — is this to persuade, inform, entertain, advise?
3. For paragraph purpose: link the paragraph to the overall argument or story

**Sentence starters for this type:**
- "The main idea of the passage is that..."
- "The writer includes this paragraph in order to..."
- "The purpose of this article is to persuade/inform/educate readers that..."

---

### Type 7: "Do You Agree?" / Personal Response Questions (2–3 marks)

**What they ask:** "Do you think [character] made the right decision? Explain your answer with reference to the text."

**Key rules:**
1. Always take a clear stance (Yes/No — do not sit on the fence)
2. Use evidence from the text to support your view
3. You may add your own reasoning, but it must be clearly linked to the text

**Example:**
- "Do you agree that Daniel should have told his parents the truth? Give reasons for your answer, referring to the text. (2 marks)"
- Correct: "Yes, I agree that Daniel should have told his parents the truth. In the text, Daniel describes feeling 'a knot of guilt in his stomach that would not go away', which shows that keeping the secret was causing him distress. Had he been honest, he would have felt relieved and his parents could have helped him resolve the situation."

## COMPONENT 5: SYNTHESIS & TRANSFORMATION (10 marks)

> **5 items × 2 marks each.** New entry in this guide (the older 215-mark spec implied S&T was absent; it is not). Each item asks you to combine and/or transform sentences while preserving the original meaning.

A dedicated KB (`SynthesisTransformation_KB.md`) will catalogue the ~25–30 recurring patterns with worked examples. The most common patterns at PSLE level are:

| Pattern | Example transformation |
|---|---|
| Conjunctions (combine into one) | *He was tired. He kept working.* → *Although he was tired, he kept working.* |
| Relative clauses | *The boy is my brother. He is wearing a red cap.* → *The boy who is wearing a red cap is my brother.* |
| If-conditionals | *Eat your vegetables, or you won't grow tall.* → *If you don't eat your vegetables, you won't grow tall.* |
| Direct ↔ Reported speech | *"I am late," she said.* → *She said that she was late.* |
| Active ↔ Passive | *The dog chased the cat.* → *The cat was chased by the dog.* |
| Too…to ↔ So…that | *He was too tired to walk.* → *He was so tired that he could not walk.* |
| Inversion (Hardly / No sooner / Scarcely) | *As soon as the bell rang, the students rushed out.* → *Hardly had the bell rung when the students rushed out.* |
| Despite / Although | *Although it was raining, the match continued.* → *Despite the rain, the match continued.* |

### Three rules every S&T answer must follow

1. **Original meaning preserved.** Do not add new information; do not change the meaning by softening or strengthening claims.
2. **Grammar perfect.** No tense slip, no SVA error, no article error. One grammar error in an S&T item often costs the full 2 marks.
3. **Use the given word / starter exactly as instructed.** If the prompt says "Begin: Hardly had…", your sentence must start exactly with *Hardly had*.

### Common S&T pitfalls

| Pitfall | Fix |
|---|---|
| Adding extra information | Translate, do not expand. |
| Tense shift in reported speech | *am → was*, *is → was*, *will → would*, *can → could*, *have → had*. |
| Pronoun shift in reported speech | *"I am tired," he said.* → *He said that he was tired.* (not *I was tired*) |
| Missing the word "by" in passive | *The cake was eaten by Tom.* |
| Wrong article after inversion | *Hardly had **the** bell rung…* (not *a bell rung*) |
| Comma splice in despite/although | After *Despite*, follow with a noun phrase, not a clause. *Despite the rain* ✓ / *Despite it was raining* ✗ |

## Top 10 Comprehension Mistakes and How to Fix Them

| Mistake | Fix |
|---------|-----|
| 1. Not using RACE for 2-mark questions | ALWAYS: Restate + Answer + Quote + Explain |
| 2. Quoting too much of the passage | Quote only the most relevant phrase (1–2 lines max) |
| 3. Not finding evidence for inferences | Every inference needs a text quote |
| 4. Copying full sentences instead of answering the question | Answer the question FIRST, then support with quote |
| 5. Using vague emotion words (happy/sad) | Use precise vocabulary (elated/devastated) |
| 6. Ignoring the second part of a 2-part question | Read the question twice; answer BOTH parts |
| 7. Not answering in full sentences for 2+ mark questions | Always write in full sentences |
| 8. Misreading vocabulary-in-context | Read whole sentence + sentence before/after |
| 9. Giving personal opinion when text evidence is needed | Anchor every point to the text |
| 10. Rushing the language use questions | Always name technique + explain effect |
