---

## How to use this KB

- **Frequency tag** — how often the idiom turns up in PSLE-level reading or in marked compositions.
  - `common` = appears regularly; safe to use.
  - `moderate` = appears occasionally; use carefully and only if context fits.
  - `rare` = uncommon; impressive when right, awkward when wrong.
- **Use context** — where the idiom is most natural.
  - `composition` = strong in narrative writing.
  - `SBC` = natural in spoken oral answers.
  - `both` = works in either.
  - `avoid` = better not used at PSLE level (too colloquial or too easily mis-applied).
- **Rule of thumb:** **1–2 idioms per Continuous Writing piece** (more sounds forced); **0–1 per Situational Writing** (only if register permits and the meaning is exact); **1 per SBC turn** at most, and only if it lands naturally.

## §13 — Idioms to use very sparingly or avoid at PSLE

| Idiom | Why caution |
|---|---|
| *kick the bucket* (= die) | Inappropriately flippant; never use for a death scene |
| *piece of work* (= unpleasant person) | Negative tone, ambiguous; misread by examiners |
| *for crying out loud* | Exclamation; conversational only |
| *what the heck* | Mild slang; not standard |
| *get on (someone's) nerves* | Acceptable but use *irritate / annoy* in formal pieces |
| *a no-brainer* | Slang register |
| *bite (someone's) head off* | Violent imagery; can be misread |
| *over my dead body* | Hyperbolic; rarely appropriate |
| *raining cats and dogs* | Cliched at PSLE level; *heavy rain* or *the rain poured down in sheets* reads more maturely |

## §14 — Integration into composition (worked example)

**Topic:** Write a story about a time you had to make a difficult choice.

**Idiom plan:** one well-placed idiom — *at a crossroads* (§4) — used near the climax.

> *Standing at the gate of the football pitch, I felt as if I were standing at a crossroads. My best friend Razif was waiting for me; my mother was waiting at home with the news she had not yet found the words to share. Whichever way I walked, someone I loved would feel let down.*

**Why this works:** the idiom serves the moment; it isn't decoration. The narrator's choice is genuinely between two paths, so the metaphor fits. One idiom — landed.

## §15 — Integration into SBC (worked example)

**Q3 (Critical Opinion):** "Do you think young people today value friendship as much as previous generations did?"

> *"I can understand why some adults feel that friendships today are shallower because so much of it happens through screens. However, I think the value is still there — just expressed differently. **A friend in need is a friend indeed**, and I have seen this in real time during exam season, when my classmates and I set up a group to share notes and check on each other almost daily. The medium has changed, but the meaning hasn't."*

**Why this works:** the idiom comes in to support a specific personal example. It is not used to "show off"; it summarises the point.
