# Show, Don't Tell — Paired Examples Library
### PSLE English Language — Primary 6 | AL1 Craft

> **Status:** Authored 2026-05-13 as a sibling to `Composition_Writing_Guide.md` and `Master_PSLE_EL_KB.md`. ≥45 paired examples across 9 emotions/states + composition body cues. This KB is the single most-used reference for upgrading AL2 narrative writing to AL1.

---

## Why "show, don't tell" decides AL1

AL2 writes: *I was scared.*
AL1 writes: *My knees trembled uncontrollably while my heart pounded wildly against my chest.*

Same emotion. Different score. The difference is **showing the body, the action, the sensory detail, or the thought** instead of *naming* the emotion. PSLE examiners reward this in Continuous Writing Content marks and in language-use SBC answers.

### The F.A.S.T. lens (from `Master_PSLE_EL_KB.md`)

When you have written a "telling" sentence, ask the four F.A.S.T. questions:

| Letter | Question | Example (Fear) |
|---|---|---|
| **F**ace | What does the face do? | eyes widened, jaw clenched, lips trembled |
| **A**ctions | What does the body do? | hands shook, feet rooted to the spot, breath quickened |
| **S**peech | What is said or whispered? | "I…I can't," she stammered |
| **T**houghts | What runs through the mind? | every story Mum had ever told me about strangers came flooding back |

Any **one** of the four lifts a flat line. **Two** is AL1.

## §1 — Fear / Anxiety (≥7 pairs)

| Telling (AL2) | Showing (AL1) |
|---|---|
| I was scared. | My knees trembled uncontrollably while my heart pounded wildly against my chest. |
| She was nervous. | Her sweaty palms tightened around the microphone as her stomach twisted into knots. |
| He was terrified. | The blood drained from his face and his throat closed up; he could not have screamed even if he had wanted to. |
| I felt anxious. | I caught myself biting the inside of my cheek again, the same nervous tic I thought I had outgrown years ago. |
| She was afraid to speak. | She opened her mouth, but the words stayed lodged somewhere behind her teeth. |
| He felt uneasy walking home in the dark. | Every shadow stretched a little too far, and the click of his own footsteps sounded too loud against the empty road. |
| I was so scared I couldn't move. | My feet had become stones; I could see the exit, but my legs refused to listen. |

## §2 — Sadness / Disappointment (≥7 pairs)

| Telling (AL2) | Showing (AL1) |
|---|---|
| I was sad. | Tears blurred my vision while disappointment weighed heavily upon my chest. |
| She was heartbroken. | She held the letter in her lap for a long time, smoothing the same corner over and over again as if she could iron the news out of it. |
| He felt let down. | He stared at the trophy on the shelf — his brother's, not his — and quietly closed the bedroom door behind him. |
| I was disappointed by my results. | I read the slip once, then a second time, just to be sure that the number on the page was the one meant for me. |
| She felt lonely at her new school. | At recess, she pretended to study, because the act of pretending hurt less than sitting at a table by herself. |
| He missed his grandmother. | The smell of pandan from the kitchen always made him forget, just for a second, that she was no longer there to call him in for tea. |
| I cried for a long time. | I cried until my eyes ached and my pillow had turned a darker shade of grey. |

## §3 — Happiness / Pride / Relief (≥7 pairs)

| Telling (AL2) | Showing (AL1) |
|---|---|
| I was happy. | A grin spread across my face as warmth spread through my heart. |
| She was overjoyed. | She let out a small laugh that was half a sob, and pressed her hands to her cheeks as if to check that this was real. |
| He was proud. | He stood a little straighter as the headmaster called his name, and for the first time in his life, he did not look at the floor. |
| I felt relieved. | I let out a breath I had not realised I had been holding for the last fifteen minutes. |
| She was thrilled. | Her eyes lit up so brightly that even her grandfather, who rarely smiled, found himself smiling back. |
| He was happy his team won. | He flung his arms around his captain and shouted into the wind, his throat raw and his cheeks aching. |
| I was glad to be home. | The familiar smell of mother's curry hit me at the doorway, and the day's exhaustion fell away in an instant. |

## §4 — Anger / Frustration (≥6 pairs)

| Telling (AL2) | Showing (AL1) |
|---|---|
| He was angry. | His jaw tightened and his fists clenched until the knuckles whitened. |
| She was furious. | She set the teacup down so carefully it was almost worse than smashing it; the silence afterwards was a warning. |
| I was frustrated. | I shoved the textbook to the side and pressed the heels of my hands against my eyes. |
| He was annoyed with his sister. | He inhaled sharply through his nose and counted, silently, the way Mum had taught him to. |
| She was indignant. | "Excuse me," she said, in a voice so even and so cold that the cashier began to apologise before she had even finished her sentence. |
| I was so angry I couldn't speak. | I felt a heat climb my neck and settle behind my ears, and I knew that anything I said would come out wrong. |

|---|---|

| Telling (AL2) | Showing (AL1) |
|---|---|

| She was ashamed. | She could not meet her mother's eyes; instead, she stared at the small chip on the rim of the rice bowl. |

## §9 — Setting and atmosphere — "show, don't tell" beyond emotions (≥6 pairs)

This is the often-forgotten use of the principle. Settings can be told (*The room was messy*) or shown (*Clothes made a topography of their own; empty glasses stood on the sill like an abandoned city*).

| Telling | Showing |
|---|---|
| The room was messy. | Clothes made a topography of their own; empty glasses stood on the sill like an abandoned city. |
| It was very hot. | The air shimmered above the tarmac; even the pigeons had retreated under the shade of the bus stop. |
| It was raining heavily. | The rain came down in long, silver sheets, and the gutters had already given up trying to swallow it. |
| The hall was crowded. | We squeezed between shoulders and elbows; the air was thick with the smell of perfume and damp jackets. |
| It was quiet in the library. | The only sound was the slow turning of pages and the occasional cough that someone tried, and failed, to muffle. |
| It was a sad funeral. | No one had thought to play music; only the rustle of the curtains and the low, even hum of the air conditioner filled the room. |

## §10 — Dialogue substitutes for emotion (≥3 examples)

Often the strongest "show" is a line of dialogue.

| Telling | Showing through dialogue |
|---|---|
| She was worried about me. | "Have you eaten?" she asked, before she even said hello. |
| He was angry. | "Just leave it," he said quietly. "Just leave it where it is." |
| I was disappointed. | "It's fine," I said. It wasn't, but neither of us wanted to talk about it. |

## §11 — The "show, don't tell" upgrade table (one-glance reference)

Use when revising a draft. Search your composition for the left column; replace with the right.

| If you wrote… | Try… |
|---|---|
| I was happy / sad / angry / scared | one body action + one inner thought |
| It was a lovely day | one specific sensory detail (smell, sound, light) |
| She was a kind person | one small action that shows kindness |
| It was very tense | something that *should* have made a sound but didn't |
| The food was delicious | one specific texture/temperature/smell + one reaction |
| Everyone clapped | identify *one* person whose response was unexpected |
| He was a good friend | one small thing he did when nobody was watching |
| The day passed quickly | what was supposed to happen at the end of the day, but didn't, because time had run out |

## §12 — When NOT to "show"

"Show, don't tell" is a default, not a law. Sometimes telling is right:

- **For pace.** *Three weeks passed.* is correct. You do not need to show three weeks.
- **For minor details that don't matter.** *We took the bus home.* is fine if the bus is not part of the story.
- **For exposition the reader needs quickly.** *Sarah was my older sister.* is faster than showing.

The rule is: **show what matters; tell what doesn't.** Examiners reward selective showing, not relentless showing. A composition where every sentence "shows" is exhausting and often disjointed.

## §13 — Drills

For a tutoring app or workbook:

1. **Rewrite drill.** Given a "telling" sentence, write three different "showing" versions (one using F, one using A, one using T from F.A.S.T.).
2. **Spot-the-tell drill.** Given a 200-word draft, mark every "telling" sentence and rewrite the three most important ones.
3. **Sense-check drill.** Given a setting (e.g., *the hawker centre at dusk*), write three lines: one sight, one sound, one smell.
4. **Dialogue-as-emotion drill.** Given an emotion, write one line of dialogue that conveys it without naming the emotion.

### Guilt, dread, internal storm (from Mentor Text 1, *"A Day I Will Never Forget"*)

| Telling (AL2) | Showing (Mentor Text 1, AL1+) |
|---|---|
| I felt guilty. | *"My hands were knotted in my lap, my heart was a cold and leaden thing in my chest. The weather might have been gloriously bright, but a storm — silent, merciless — had been raging inside me for three days."* |
| I felt sick when I realised. | *"My stomach had lurched with a dread so acute it was almost physical."* |
| The lie was eating me up. | *"The lie of silence was eating me alive, slowly and thoroughly, the way rust devours iron."* |
| I felt out of place at the beach. | *"To everyone around me, it was paradise. To me, it felt like wading through fog. My feet dragged against the pavement. A familiar heaviness pressed behind my eyes."* |
| I felt very afraid when he called. | *"With shaking fingers, I answered… My heart lurched into my throat. I stood motionless as the waves lapped softly around my ankles."* |
| The truth hit me hard. | *"The words struck me like a physical blow. I felt the blood drain from my face. My legs buckled; I sat down heavily in the wet sand."* |
| I felt small and unreachable. | *"All of it seemed impossibly far away, as though I were watching it through thick glass."* |
| He looked at me differently. | *"There was something in his eyes that had not been there before — a cautious, considering respect that felt, to me, more valuable than easy forgiveness."* |

### Hospital waking, calm under crisis (from Mentor Text 2, *"The Bravest Thing"*)

| Telling | Showing (Mentor Text 2) |
|---|---|
| I woke up in hospital. | *"The first thing I noticed when I regained consciousness was the smell — antiseptic, sharp and clinical, cutting through the fog in my mind like a blade. White ceiling tiles swam into focus above me. The steady, metronome beeping of a heart monitor filled the sterile room."* |
| My mum was relieved. | *"The relief in her voice was so profound and raw that it almost undid me."* |
| I was tired and sore. | *"My whole body felt as though it had been wrung out like a wet cloth."* |
| I felt nervous about the slope. | *"I felt a flicker of unease, cool and instinctive, at the base of my stomach."* |
| I was scared before going down. | *"At the top of the slope, the full severity of the incline became apparent — a long, smooth ribbon of concrete that plunged steeply and curved sharply at the bottom into a blind corner lined with concrete bollards."* (showing fear through what the eye notices) |
| I crashed and blacked out. | *"The impact, when it came, was a white-hot explosion of pain that obliterated all conscious thought. I was dimly aware of tumbling — of concrete scraping against skin, of the bicycle clattering violently away from me — and then everything went very still and very dark."* |
| Priya was scared but did the right thing. | *"She had spoken to me in a calm, steady voice throughout, even as her own tears fell silently and continuously down her cheeks."* |
| She admitted she was scared. | *"'I was absolutely terrified,' she admitted quietly. 'That,' I told her, 'is exactly what makes it brave.'"* |

### Exam-hall anxiety and resolve (from Mentor Text 3, *"Never Give Up"*)

| Telling | Showing (Mentor Text 3) |
|---|---|
| The weather was grey and gloomy. | *"The sky on the morning of the Inter-School Mathematics Olympiad was the colour of ash. A thick, unbroken grey pressed down over the city like the lid of a pot, swallowing the horizon, muffling sound, draining colour from the streets below."* |
| I felt convinced I would fail. | *"I felt, with a desolate certainty, that the weather had arranged itself in perfect sympathy with how I felt: hollow, leaden, and utterly convinced that I was about to fail."* |
| Maths is hard for me. | *"While my classmates seemed to absorb formulae and theorems the way a sponge absorbs water — effortlessly, naturally, leaving no residue of doubt — I had always had to fight for every mark."* |
| The exam hall was intimidating. | *"The hall was immense — cool, white-walled, its fluorescent lights humming with a faint, colourless persistence above rows of bare tables."* |
| I panicked at the first question. | *"My heartbeat became a roar in my ears. It is over, whispered a voice from some dark corner of my mind — low, insistent, and utterly persuasive. You don't belong here. You never did."* |
| I worked very hard. | *"Slowly, torturously, as if I were tunnelling through solid rock with my bare hands, I worked my way through the paper… Perspiration beaded at my temples despite the cool hum of the air-conditioning above me."* |
| I felt happy and proud when I saw the result. | *"They were something more complicated than either: the tears of someone who had doubted herself completely, had nearly surrendered to that doubt in the most critical moment, and had discovered — in the silence between the roaring and the pencil touching the page — that she was stronger than she had believed."* |
| The sky cleared and matched my mood. | *"The oppressive, ash-grey ceiling of the morning had dissolved entirely, replaced by a deep, luminous blue — the particular, washed blue that exists only after a long grey spell has finally broken… The weather, it seemed, had finally caught up with how I felt."* |

### Singapore-specific sensory cues to mine

The mentor texts use these recognisably-Singaporean sensory anchors. Reuse them when the topic permits:

- *mynah bird's two-note call*
- *salt-tang of the sea*
- *roller-coaster screams from Sentosa*
- *angsana trees lining the school driveway*
- *the smell of freshly cut grass*
- *ang pow money* (as the source of the protagonist's savings)
- *void-deck quiet at dusk* (implied)
- *fluorescent lights humming above exam-hall tables*

### Mentor-craft note — emotion through environment

The richest "show, don't tell" technique in the mentor texts is **environment-as-emotion-mirror**, used through the Weather Contrast pattern:

- *bright sunshine* mirroring inner *guilt* (MT1)
- *ash-grey sky* mirroring inner *dread* (MT3)
- *afternoon light soft and golden* in the recovery hospital scene (MT2)

When a student writes *"the sun was out"*, push them to ask: *what is your character's emotion right now, and how can the weather either match or ironically contrast it?* This single move tends to lift Continuous Writing one band.
