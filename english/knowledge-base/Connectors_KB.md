# Connectors and Discourse Markers — Knowledge Base
### PSLE English Language — Primary 6

> **Status:** Authored 2026-05-13. Extends the small connector list inside `Vocabulary_Idioms_Bank.md` (which lists ~20 transition words) to **≥60 connectors across 8 functions**, with usage notes, position rules, and register guidance. Designed for Composition (both Situational and Continuous Writing), Comprehension OE answers (especially language-use explanations), and Oral SBC.

---

## Why connectors matter at PSLE

Connectors are the **invisible scaffolding** of a high-band piece. AL1 narratives and arguments do not feel "lumpy" because connectors weld ideas together. AL2 writing often reads as a list of sentences in chronological order; AL1 writing reads as one continuous argument or scene.

- **Continuous Writing.** Connectors win marks in Language & Organisation. Stronger transitions between paragraphs and within sentences signal coherent thinking.
- **Situational Writing.** Connectors clarify each bullet point's logical relationship to the next, especially in articles, speeches, and reports.
- **Comprehension OE.** Language-use answers ("explain the effect of…") need cohesive prose. A connector turns *The writer uses a simile. This creates urgency.* into *The writer uses a simile, which creates a sense of urgency for the reader.*
- **Oral SBC.** Connectors elevate spoken answers from list-style to argument-style. PEEL and VBP both rely on the right transitions.

## §1 — Adding / building on a point (≥8)

Use these to add a second or third reason, example, or fact in the same direction.

| Connector | Position | Register | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| **In addition** | sentence opener (with comma) | mid-formal–formal | *In addition, the library will extend its opening hours.* |
| **Furthermore** | sentence opener | formal | *Furthermore, the school plans to introduce a recycling programme.* |
| **Moreover** | sentence opener | formal | *Moreover, the new rule will reduce paper waste.* |
| **What is more** | sentence opener | mid-formal | *What is more, parents have responded positively.* |
| **Also** | mid-sentence | informal–mid-formal | *The hall is also used for assemblies.* |
| **Besides** | sentence opener or mid-sentence | mid-formal | *Besides, the cost is low.* |
| **Not only … but also …** | balanced clauses | mid-formal–formal | *Not only does the programme save energy, but it also teaches responsibility.* |
| **As well as** | followed by noun or -ing | mid-formal | *As well as reducing waste, recycling teaches discipline.* |

## §2 — Contrasting / opposing ideas (≥10)

Use these to introduce a counter-point, a limitation, or a surprise.

| Connector | Position | Register | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| **However** | sentence opener (with comma) | formal | *However, the team did not give up.* |
| **Nevertheless** | sentence opener | formal | *Nevertheless, the journey continued.* |
| **Nonetheless** | sentence opener | formal | *Nonetheless, she finished the race.* |
| **On the other hand** | sentence opener | mid-formal–formal | *On the other hand, online learning has its drawbacks.* |
| **In contrast** | sentence opener | formal | *In contrast, the older method took twice as long.* |
| **By contrast** | sentence opener | formal | *By contrast, my brother prefers football.* |
| **Conversely** | sentence opener | formal | *Conversely, some students benefit from extra practice.* |
| **Although** | begins a clause | mid-formal–formal | *Although it was raining, the match continued.* |
| **Even though** | begins a clause | mid-formal | *Even though he was tired, he kept working.* |
| **Whereas** | mid-sentence | mid-formal–formal | *I prefer reading, whereas my sister prefers painting.* |
| **While** (in the sense of "whereas") | mid-sentence | mid-formal | *Some students enjoy mathematics, while others find it difficult.* |
| **Despite** + noun/gerund | sentence opener | formal | *Despite the rain, the football match continued.* |
| **In spite of** + noun/gerund | sentence opener | formal | *In spite of his fear, he stepped onto the stage.* |
| **Yet** | sentence opener | mid-formal | *Yet the most important lesson came afterwards.* |
| **But** | mid-sentence (rarely opener) | informal–mid-formal | *I was tired, but I kept going.* |

> **PSLE tip.** *Despite* must be followed by a noun phrase or gerund, NOT a clause. *Despite the rain* ✓ / *Despite it was raining* ✗. This is the single most-tested S&T transformation involving connectors.

## §3 — Cause / reason (≥7)

| Connector | Position | Register | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Because** | begins a clause | informal–mid-formal | *I stayed in because it was raining.* |
| **As** (in the sense of "because") | begins a clause | mid-formal | *As the storm grew, we sheltered under the bridge.* |
| **Since** (in the sense of "because") | begins a clause | mid-formal | *Since you are here, we can start.* |
| **Due to** + noun/gerund | sentence opener or mid | formal | *Due to the heavy rain, the match was cancelled.* |
| **Owing to** + noun/gerund | sentence opener | formal | *Owing to the noise, the talk was rescheduled.* |
| **Thanks to** + noun/gerund | sentence opener | mid-formal | *Thanks to her quick thinking, no one was hurt.* |
| **On account of** + noun | mid-sentence | formal | *The flight was delayed on account of the weather.* |

## §4 — Result / consequence (≥7)

| Connector | Position | Register | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Therefore** | sentence opener (with comma) | formal | *Therefore, the team had to start over.* |
| **Thus** | sentence opener or mid | formal | *Thus, the rule was changed.* |
| **Hence** | sentence opener | formal | *Hence, the decision was inevitable.* |
| **Consequently** | sentence opener | formal | *Consequently, attendance dropped.* |
| **As a result** | sentence opener | mid-formal–formal | *As a result, traffic was diverted.* |
| **For this reason** | sentence opener | mid-formal–formal | *For this reason, the campaign was launched.* |
| **So** | mid-sentence (informal) | informal | *It was late, so we went home.* |

> **PSLE tip.** *Therefore* and *thus* are powerful but easy to overuse. Use one per paragraph maximum.

## §5 — Sequence / time (≥10)

These are essential for Continuous Writing pacing.

| Connector | Position | Register | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| **First / Firstly** | paragraph opener | mid-formal–formal | *Firstly, the team gathered at the entrance.* |
| **Second / Secondly** | paragraph opener | mid-formal–formal | *Secondly, they checked their equipment.* |
| **Next** | sentence opener | informal–mid-formal | *Next, we walked towards the bridge.* |
| **Then** | sentence opener or mid | informal | *Then the lights went out.* |
| **After that** | sentence opener | mid-formal | *After that, the rain began to fall.* |
| **Afterwards** | sentence opener | mid-formal | *Afterwards, we went home in silence.* |
| **Meanwhile** | sentence opener | mid-formal–formal | *Meanwhile, my brother was waiting at the gate.* |
| **At the same time** | sentence opener | mid-formal | *At the same time, the band started to play.* |
| **Eventually** | sentence opener or mid | mid-formal–formal | *Eventually, the storm passed.* |
| **Soon** | sentence opener | informal–mid-formal | *Soon, the crowd began to gather.* |
| **Suddenly** | sentence opener | mid-formal | *Suddenly, the door swung open.* |
| **Without warning** | sentence opener | mid-formal | *Without warning, the lights cut out.* |
| **Before long** | sentence opener | mid-formal | *Before long, the rain had soaked everything.* |
| **Finally / At last** | sentence opener | mid-formal–formal | *Finally, the bus arrived.* |
| **In the end** | sentence opener | mid-formal | *In the end, we walked home together.* |

> **PSLE tip.** Avoid the chain *First… Then… After that… Finally…* in a single paragraph. Vary the order with sense-of-time phrases: *That afternoon, Three weeks later, Without thinking, Before I could answer*.

These appear in SBC Q3 (VBP framework) and in articles. They signal maturity.

| Connector | Position | Register | Example |

| **Of course** | sentence opener | mid-formal | *Of course, not everyone agrees with this view.* |
| **Granted** | sentence opener | formal | *Granted, the path is long, but the destination is worth it.* |
| **While I understand that** | sentence opener | mid-formal | *While I understand that screens can be distracting, they are also useful learning tools.* |

## §8 — Concluding / summarising (≥6)

| Connector | Position | Register | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| **In conclusion** | paragraph opener | mid-formal–formal | *In conclusion, recycling is everyone's responsibility.* |
| **To conclude** | paragraph opener | formal | *To conclude, the programme has been a great success.* |
| **To sum up** | paragraph opener | mid-formal | *To sum up, three changes are needed.* |
| **In summary** | paragraph opener | formal | *In summary, the campaign achieved its three goals.* |
| **All in all** | paragraph opener | mid-formal | *All in all, the trip was memorable.* |
| **Overall** | paragraph opener or mid | mid-formal–formal | *Overall, the response from the community was encouraging.* |
| **Ultimately** | sentence opener | mid-formal–formal | *Ultimately, the decision is ours to make.* |

## §10 — Position rules (where to put a connector)

A connector at the **start** of a sentence is almost always followed by a comma:
*However, the rain did not stop.* ✓
*However the rain did not stop.* ✗

A connector in the **middle** of a sentence is usually surrounded by commas:
*The library, moreover, is free to enter.* ✓

A subordinating connector (*although, because, since, while, when, before, after, if, unless*) begins a **subordinate clause** and the comma goes before the main clause:
*Although it was raining, the match continued.* ✓
*The match continued although it was raining.* ✓ (no comma before *although* when it follows the main clause)

## §11 — Register notes (formal vs informal)

For a formal letter or article:
- Prefer: *Furthermore, Moreover, Nevertheless, Consequently, In addition, Despite, In contrast, In conclusion, Ultimately*
- Avoid as openers: *So, And, But* (use mid-sentence with commas if needed)

For an informal email or narrative dialogue:
- Free to use: *And, But, So, Also, Anyway, Plus, Besides*
- Avoid: *Moreover, Hence, Consequently* (feel too stiff)

For oral SBC:
- Best: *In addition, For example, On the other hand, At the same time, In my view, Ultimately*
- Avoid in speech: *Moreover, Hence, Thus* (sound bookish; use *Also, So, Therefore* instead)

## §12 — Common errors and fixes

| Wrong | Fix | Why |
|---|---|---|
| *Although it was raining, but the match continued.* | *Although it was raining, the match continued.* OR *It was raining, but the match continued.* | *Although* and *but* are double-up; use one |
| *Despite of the rain, the match continued.* | *Despite the rain, the match continued.* | *Despite* does NOT take *of* |
| *In spite the rain, the match continued.* | *In spite of the rain, the match continued.* | *In spite* DOES take *of* |
| *Because of he was tired, he stopped.* | *Because he was tired, he stopped.* OR *Because of his tiredness, he stopped.* | *Because* takes a clause; *because of* takes a noun |
| *I went home. Beause I was tired.* | *I went home because I was tired.* | *Because* should not start a sentence in formal writing |
| *Moreover, also, the school has expanded.* | *Moreover, the school has expanded.* OR *Also, the school has expanded.* | Don't stack two adding connectors |
| *In conclusion, finally, I would like to say…* | *In conclusion, I would like to say…* | Don't stack two concluding connectors |
| *He was tired, however, he kept working.* | *He was tired; however, he kept working.* OR *He was tired. However, he kept working.* | *However* requires either a semicolon or a full stop before it, not a comma |
| *And, the rain continued.* | *The rain continued.* OR *Furthermore, the rain continued.* | Don't start a sentence with *And* in formal writing |

## §13 — Drills for tutoring app or workbook

1. **Insert-the-connector drill.** Two unconnected sentences → student inserts the most appropriate connector.
2. **Replace-the-overused drill.** A paragraph using *Then* four times → student replaces three of them with varied alternatives.
3. **Function-tagging drill.** Given a paragraph, tag every connector with its function (adding, contrasting, causal, sequencing, etc.).
4. **Position-fix drill.** Sentences with wrong connector position → student moves the connector to the correct position and adds the comma.
