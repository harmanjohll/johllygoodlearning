How to actually study English

The goal is not to feel busy. The goal is to remember when it matters — in the exam hall, six months from now. These strategies are evidence-backed (Bjork, Clear, Dweck, McGonigal) and built for AL1.

Memory and recall

1. Retrieve, don't reread

Re-reading your notes is one of the weakest study strategies, even though it feels productive. Retrieval — trying to remember without looking — is the strongest. Close the Learn tab and write down what you remember before you check.

2. Space it out

Don't drill one topic for 60 minutes. Review it today, tomorrow, in 4 days, in a week, in a month. The Idioms SRS does this for idioms automatically. The Review queue surfaces topics that are slipping.

Try this now → Idioms SRS → Review queue

3. Interleave topics

Don't spend a whole session on Continuous Writing. Mix two or three topics in one session — composition + comprehension + vocab. The brain finds the patterns better when they appear in mixed order. Use the Compass to plan a mixed session.

Writing and composition

4. The Feynman test

If you cannot explain something simply, you do not understand it. After you finish a Continuous Writing piece, summarise the whole story in one sentence. If the summary is vague, the story is too.

Try this now → Composition

5. Get examiner feedback before you ask Mum

The Composition Builder marks your draft against the SEAB 2025+ rubric in 10 seconds. Use it first; then refine. Don't ask a human reviewer to fix mechanical errors — that's the AI's job. Save the human review for craft and meaning.

Time and energy

7. Show up, even when you don't want to

The hardest part of studying is the first 90 seconds. Once you are in, you are in. Never miss twice — missing one day is noise; missing two becomes a pattern.

Try this now → Compass

8. Use the 20-minute rule

Twenty focused minutes is better than two hours of half-attention. Set a timer (use Pomodoro, when it ships). Start small. Stop when the timer goes off, even if you feel like continuing — that's how you make tomorrow easier.

Try this now → Compass (15 min option)

9. Sleep on it

Eight hours tonight does more for tomorrow's recall than one more hour now. Memory consolidates during sleep — that is when today's practice becomes tomorrow's fluency. If it is past 22:00, stop.

Mindset

10. Praise strategy, not ability

"I'm bad at English" is fixed-mindset talk. "I haven't drilled the synthesis & transformation patterns yet" is growth-mindset talk. They describe the same situation. Only the second one tells you what to do next.

11. Stress is a feature, not a bug

When you feel your heart racing before a quiz, that is your body mobilising — pumping oxygen to your brain, sharpening your focus. The exam-day nerves you feel are the same nerves an athlete feels before a race. Reframe the feeling, don't fight it.

12. The Mirror tells the truth

When you wonder if you are improving, open the Mirror. Not to gloat or panic — just to see. Four indicators, not one percentage. Effort is its own story, separate from performance. On a hard day, showing up is enough.

Paper-specific tactics (from the KBs)

13. For Paper 1 Continuous Writing — read the three mentor essays before drafting

ModelEssays_Annotated_KB.md has three Band-5 mentor essays with paragraph-by-paragraph annotations: "A Day I Will Never Forget" (Weather Contrast), "The Bravest Thing" (Flashback), "Never Give Up" (Weather Contrast at higher difficulty). Pick whichever technique fits today's topic, then ape its structure. You cannot write like AL1 until you have read AL1.

Try this now → Continuous Writing → Composition Builder (Examiner mode)

14. For Paper 1 Situational Writing — number the six points before you draft

The single biggest mark loss in Situational Writing is missing one of the six content points — especially the underlined critical-thinking point whose information is NOT in the stimulus. Before writing a single sentence: count to six on the stimulus, mark them, identify the underlined one, and invent a specific realistic detail for it (not "it is good").

Try this now → Situational Writing

15. For Paper 2 Comprehension OE — RACE on every 2+ mark question, every time

Restate the question. Answer directly. Cite the exact quote in quotation marks. Explain how the quote supports your answer. Skip any letter and you cap your mark. The Comprehension Strategy Guide has worked RACE examples for every question type.

Try this now → Comprehension OE → Builder Examiner for comprehension

16. For Paper 2 Cloze — never write the answer before reading the whole passage

Cloze is built so context across multiple sentences decides each blank. Read end-to-end once. Identify the tense across the passage. Then fill the easy ones first (grammar-clue blanks), then the medium (context-clue), then the hard (collocation-clue). Half the lost marks here are spelling — double-check every word you write.

Try this now → Comprehension Cloze

17. For Paper 2 Synthesis & Transformation — start with the given word

If the prompt says "Begin: Hardly had…", your first word must be Hardly. If it says "Use: despite", you must use Despite followed by a noun phrase (not a clause). The eight high-frequency patterns are drillable. One slip = full 2 marks lost.

Try this now → Synthesis & Transformation

18. For Paper 4 SBC Q3 — validate the opposing view FIRST

The Q3 (critical opinion) question rewards intellectual honesty. Validate ("I can understand why some people feel…"), Bridge ("However, when we consider…"), Persuade ("for example, when I…"). Examiners hear hundreds of "Yes I think so because…" — the V step makes you stand out.

Try this now → SBC

19. For Paper 4 Reading Aloud — read the preamble first, decide the tone

The preamble (new in 2025) tells you who is speaking and why. Use PACT: Purpose, Audience, Context, Tone. An Earth Day speech = urgent, conviction-filled. A personal recount = warm, conversational. Get the tone wrong and even perfect pronunciation cannot recover the marks.

Try this now → Reading Aloud

20. For Paper 3 Listening — predict before you listen

Time is given before each audio to read the question stem and four options. Use it. Predict what kind of information you are listening for. Then use the FIRST listen to identify; the SECOND to confirm. Don't restart your thinking on the second listen — you waste it.

Try this now → Listening Comprehension
