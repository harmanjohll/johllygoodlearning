🔥 What is Heat?

Heat is a form of energy that flows from a hotter object to a cooler object. Temperature is a measure of how hot or cold an object is — it does not tell us the total energy stored.

  • Heat always flows from higher temperature → lower temperature
  • Temperature is measured in degrees Celsius (°C)
  • A large bucket of warm water has more heat energy than a small cup of boiling water — even though the cup has a higher temperature
🌡 Key distinction: Heat is energy transferred; Temperature is a measure of hotness. They are NOT the same!

🔩 Conduction

Conduction is the transfer of heat through direct contact. When one end of a metal rod is heated, its particles vibrate faster and pass energy to neighbouring particles, gradually spreading the heat.

  • Only occurs through direct contact — no material movement
  • Metals are good conductors: copper > aluminium > iron
  • Non-metals (wood, plastic, rubber, air) are poor conductors = insulators
  • Insulators are used in pot handles, oven mitts, building walls
  • Double-layered walls with trapped air reduce heat loss from buildings
Conduction: heat travels from particle to particle (solid/liquid) — no bulk movement of material

🌊 Convection

Convection is the transfer of heat through fluids (liquids and gases). When a fluid is heated, it expands, becomes less dense, and rises. Cooler, denser fluid sinks to take its place — forming a convection current.

  • Requires a fluid medium (liquid or gas)
  • Hot fluid rises ↑ (less dense) · Cool fluid sinks ↓ (more dense)
  • Sea breeze: land heats faster than sea → hot air over land rises → cool sea air flows in
  • Boiling water: hot water at bottom rises; cooler water descends
  • Room heaters work by convection — warm air rises and circulates
💡 Convection needs a fluid — it cannot occur in solids. Conduction occurs in both solids and fluids.

☀ Radiation

Radiation is the transfer of heat as infrared (thermal) radiation — electromagnetic waves. It does not need a medium and can travel through a vacuum.

  • All objects emit thermal radiation at all times
  • Hotter objects emit more radiation
  • Dark / dull surfaces absorb and emit radiation better
  • Light / shiny surfaces reflect radiation and are poor absorbers/emitters
  • The Sun heats the Earth by radiation across 150 million km of space
  • Silver emergency blankets reflect radiation to keep people warm
Dark dull = better absorber & emitter | Light shiny = better reflector

⚖ Thermal Equilibrium

When a hotter object and a cooler object are in contact (or nearby), heat flows from hot to cool until both reach the same temperature. At this point, heat transfer stops — they are in thermal equilibrium.

  • A hot drink cools until it matches room temperature
  • A cold drink warms until it matches room temperature
  • At thermal equilibrium, the net heat flow is zero

🌡 Effects of Gaining / Losing Heat

When objects gain or lose heat, two main things happen: temperature change and/or change of state.

  • Thermal expansion: solids, liquids, and gases expand when heated; contract when cooled
  • Railway tracks have expansion gaps to prevent buckling in heat
  • Thermometers use expansion of liquid mercury or alcohol
  • Melting: solid → liquid (gains heat at melting point)
  • Boiling / Evaporation: liquid → gas (gains heat at boiling point)
  • Condensation: gas → liquid (loses heat)
  • Freezing: liquid → solid (loses heat at freezing/melting point)
During a change of state, temperature stays constant even though heat is still being gained or lost.
Summary

Heat is energy that flows from hotter to cooler objects; temperature measures degree of hotness. Heat travels by three methods: conduction (direct contact — metals conduct best, non-metals insulate), convection (through fluids — hot fluid rises, cool fluid sinks, forming convection currents), and radiation (infrared waves — no medium needed; dark dull surfaces absorb and emit best). When objects reach the same temperature, thermal equilibrium is achieved and heat transfer stops.

Gaining heat causes thermal expansion (particles move further apart) and can cause changes of state: melting (solid → liquid at melting point), boiling (liquid → gas at boiling point). Losing heat causes contraction, condensation, and freezing. During a change of state, temperature stays constant while energy is absorbed or released.
