🌿 Plant Transport System

Systems  P5   Mastery: —
Key Questions
Glossary

🌿 Two transport tissues

A plant has two separate transport tissues running through its stems, leaves, and roots:

  • Xylem — carries water and mineral salts UP from the roots to the leaves. Made of dead, hollow cells stacked end-to-end to form continuous tubes.
  • Phloem — carries food (sugars made by photosynthesis) around the plant. Made of living cells. Food flows from a source (leaf) to a sink (root, growing shoot, fruit).
🔑 Water in xylem: one direction (up). Food in phloem: both directions (up or down, source → sink).

💨 Transpiration pulls water up

Transpiration is the loss of water vapour from the leaves through tiny pores called stomata. As water leaves the top, it pulls more water up from below — a continuous transpiration stream that can lift water more than 30 metres in a tall tree.

  • Water is absorbed at the root hair cells (which have lots of surface area).
  • It rises through the xylem in the stem.
  • It exits as water vapour through the stomata on the underside of the leaves.

🍬 Food: source to sink

Sugar is made in the leaves by photosynthesis. It is loaded into the phloem and sent to every part of the plant that needs energy or storage:

  • Up to flowers and growing shoots.
  • Down to the roots (for respiration and storage, e.g. potatoes).
  • Across to fruits as they swell and ripen.

🫧 Stomata control the flow

Stomata can open and close. When they open, water vapour escapes (transpiration increases) and gases can be exchanged for photosynthesis. When they shut (often at night or on hot dry days), the plant keeps hold of water but slows photosynthesis.

Summary

A plant's transport system has two tissues. Xylem carries water and mineral salts UP from the roots to the leaves; flow is one way and is driven by transpiration at the leaves. Phloem carries food from the leaves (source) to wherever it is used or stored (sink), up or down. Without this system, a tall plant could not get water to its leaves or food to its roots.

What to notice

When stomata close, transpiration slows, so the water stream up the xylem slows. Food in the phloem still moves because it is actively pumped from source to sink. Water only ever flows up in xylem; food can flow up or down in phloem.

PSLE Tip

Always name the substance AND the direction:

  • Xylem: water + mineral salts, roots to leaves
  • Phloem: food, source to sink (both ways)

Transpiration is NOT respiration.
