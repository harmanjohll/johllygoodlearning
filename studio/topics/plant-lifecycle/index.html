🌱 The Life Cycle of a Flowering Plant

A life cycle is the series of stages a living thing goes through from birth to reproduction. A flowering plant's life cycle has four main stages that repeat through every generation:

  • 1. Seed — contains a tiny embryo plus stored food in the cotyledon.
  • 2. Seedling — a young plant with first leaves; still living off the cotyledon's stored food.
  • 3. Young plant — grows leaves, roots, and stems; now makes its own food by photosynthesis.
  • 4. Adult plant — produces flowers, which are pollinated and fertilised to form new seeds.
🔑 The cycle is continuous: the adult's seeds become the next generation's starting point.

💧 Germination

Germination is the process by which a seed begins to grow into a seedling. Three conditions are needed:

  • Water — softens the seed coat and activates enzymes.
  • Air (oxygen) — allows the seed to respire and release energy.
  • Warmth — a suitable temperature for growth.

Light is NOT needed for germination itself. Light becomes important once the seedling breaks through the soil and starts photosynthesis.

🌾 Inside a Seed

A seed has three main parts:

  • Seed coat — tough outer layer that protects the seed.
  • Embryo — the tiny undeveloped plant.
  • Cotyledon — the food store that feeds the growing embryo until the seedling can make its own food.

🌸 From Flower to New Seed

An adult flowering plant produces flowers. Pollen is transferred from the anther to the stigma (pollination). The pollen nucleus then joins with an ovule (fertilisation). The fertilised ovule becomes a seed, and the ovary develops into the fruit. The seeds are dispersed, land in soil, and the cycle begins again.

🌿 Non-flowering plants

Not all plants reproduce with seeds. Non-flowering plants such as ferns and mosses reproduce using spores. Their life cycles follow different stages, but the idea of a repeating cycle is the same.

Summary

A flowering plant's life cycle has four stages: seed → seedling → young plant → adult plant, repeating because the adult produces new seeds. Germination needs water, air, and warmth (not light). A seed contains an embryo and a cotyledon that feeds the seedling until leaves appear. Non-flowering plants like ferns and mosses reproduce by spores.

What to notice

The cycle is continuous: stage 5 (seed formation) feeds back into stage 1 (a new seed). For germination you need water, air, and warmth. Light and soil nutrients are not required to trigger germination — they matter only once the seedling has pushed above the ground.

PSLE Tip

Three words that win germination questions:

  • water
  • air (or oxygen)
  • warmth

Do not write "sunlight" — examiners penalise that.
