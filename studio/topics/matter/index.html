Glossary

🧊 The three states

All matter exists in one of three states:

  • Solid — fixed shape, fixed volume. Particles are close and only vibrate.
  • Liquid — fixed volume, takes the shape of its container. Particles are loosely packed and flow.
  • Gas — no fixed shape or volume. Particles move freely and spread out to fill the container.
🔑 The particles are the same in all three states — only how they are arranged and how much they move is different.

🔥 Heating and cooling change the state

Adding heat gives particles more energy. Removing heat takes energy away.

  • Melting (solid → liquid) happens when a solid is heated past its melting point.
  • Evaporation (liquid → gas) happens at the surface at any temperature below boiling.
  • Boiling (liquid → gas) happens throughout the liquid at the boiling point.
  • Condensation (gas → liquid) happens when a gas cools.
  • Freezing (liquid → solid) happens when a liquid cools past its freezing point.

⚖️ Matter is conserved

State changes do not create or destroy matter. The same particles are still there, just arranged differently. If you could weigh the ice, the water, and the water vapour made from the same piece of ice, they would all have the same mass (as long as none escapes).

Mass before state change = Mass after state change

🎈 Gases have mass

Even though we cannot see most gases, they take up space and have mass. A balloon full of air is heavier than an empty one. Air presses on us from every direction — that is the mass of the gases in our atmosphere.

Summary

Matter has three states: solid (fixed shape and volume), liquid (fixed volume only), gas (neither fixed). Heating drives particles further apart (melting, evaporation, boiling). Cooling pulls them back together (condensation, freezing). Evaporation is at the surface at any temperature; boiling is throughout the liquid at the boiling point. No particles are created or destroyed — mass is conserved.

What to notice

The particles never change into different things — only how they are packed and how they move changes. At 0 °C ice melts to water; at 100 °C water boils throughout to vapour; below 100 °C water can still evaporate from its surface; if vapour cools it condenses back; if water cools to 0 °C or below it freezes back to ice.
